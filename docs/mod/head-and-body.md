# Head and body movement in VR

Giving the player full, comfortable control of where they look and where the character faces, driven by the tracked HMD and later the controllers. This is design plus a reverse-engineering target: the design decides how head and body yaw relate; the RE is finding where the engine writes the head bone and camera so the HMD can own them.

## The core problem

VR sickness is driven mostly by vestibular disconnect — the eyes report a rotation the inner ear never felt. Every scheme below is a different answer to how much of the character's facing (yaw) comes from the player's real head and body versus from artificial input. A second, separate concern is where the visible torso points, which is presentation and IK, not comfort. Two ergonomic limits recur: comfortable sustained head yaw is about 30°, practical maximum about 45°; the eyes turn rather than strain past about 30°.

## Yaw-coupling schemes

- **Decoupled head (A).** Body fixed, head free within a ~30–45° cone; body yaw set by movement or input. Maximally comfortable because nothing rotates the body under the player without input. The natural baseline, but rarely enough on its own for 360° facing.
- **Body-follows-head (B).** Body yaw lazily chases head yaw through a deadzone, then eases, recentering as the head returns forward. This drives the visible torso and the movement frame *only*, never the camera — feeding it to the camera injects vection. The deadzone mechanism is standard (it's VRIK's `MaxRootAngle`); the specific "torso = low-pass-filtered head yaw" angles and time-constants are engineering lore, not a documented platform spec.
- **Stick-driven body yaw (C), head decoupled on top.** Snap turn is instant, in fixed increments (45° is the de-facto default) — the safe artificial turn, since instantaneous rotation produces no optic flow and so no vection. Smooth turn is more immersive and more nauseating; ship it opt-in with limited acceleration and a comfort vignette. Snap must rotate around the *head* position, not the rig centre, with a brief fade to hide the discontinuity.
- **Physical turning (D).** Body yaw from real rotation; best possible comfort, but needs space and can't represent fast or vehicle yaw. Always honoured on top of the others.

## Recommended scheme per JC3 mode

JC3 is a fast open-world action game — shoot, melee, drive, fly, wingsuit, grapple — and is inherently intense; there's no pretending it into a "Comfortable" VR experience, and the realistic audience is players who already have strong VR legs. So the defaults lean immersive — smooth turning, minimal vignette — and the comfort/safety features below (snap turn, vignettes, wider deadzones) are catalogued as work to add later, not shipped on by default. The taxonomy is here so we know what those later options are, not as a prescription to enable them up front.

**On-foot.** Head fully decoupled and 1:1 (A), free within the cone, never clamped — hand off to body catch-up at the edge rather than clamping. Body yaw stick-driven (C), **default smooth turn**; snap turn and a comfort vignette are opt-in extras to build later. Visible torso follows the head (B), deadzone ~15–30°, driving the torso and movement frame only. Locomotion-forward defaults to head-relative (expose hand-relative). Physical turning always honoured.

**Vehicles** — the easy case, because the body root is fixed, so you skip body-yaw decoupling entirely. Head-look 1:1 and free; do *not* clamp it (breaking 1:1 is itself a discomfort source, and the seated pose self-limits travel). Recenter is a manual, bindable button — no auto-snap-back. Decoupled head-look, not look-to-steer: steering stays on the stick, and looking out the side window must not turn the vehicle. No VR snap-turn needed (the real neck covers it). Keep an earth-stable horizon — don't roll it with the vehicle — and treat the cockpit interior as a free rest frame (a comfort win that costs nothing and breaks no immersion). A stronger vignette for planes and helis is a later comfort option, not a default.

**Wingsuit / parachute** — the hardest, most provocative mode, with high continuous speed, banking, and constant optic flow, and no strong shipped precedent, so treat this as a hypothesis to playtest rather than settled practice. Lock body yaw to the flight/travel direction (the steering is the body banking; don't also let the stick free-spin it). Keep the head fully decoupled and 1:1 — the headline feature is looking around while you fly where you're pointed. A vignette keyed to speed and angular velocity is the single highest-value comfort feature to build for this mode (banking is high angular velocity and peak conflict), but like the rest it's a later addition, not a default. Camera roll follows the wingsuit bank by default — the most immersive choice, consistent with the intense-by-default stance — even though the research flags rolling the camera with the bank as a top sickness source; a roll-stabilised horizon is the opt-in comfort feature to layer on later if it proves necessary. Grapple yanks are sudden accelerations worth a fade or vignette-spike once that machinery exists.

## Body-from-head heuristic

JC3 has a real rig, so there's a known body bone — but you still decide how its yaw relates to the HMD. The layered estimate: body yaw is a damped follow of head yaw with a deadzone (~10° free, catch-up over ~30° / 0.2–0.5 s — the Cyberpunk third-person-mod free-range/catch-up pattern, mechanically VRIK's `MaxRootAngle`); a small hand-midpoint correction weighted low, since hands move wildly; and the locomotion-forward frame decoupled from both gaze and torso. Drive the existing body bone via scheme B rather than building a physics body.

## HMD pose to camera

The transform chain, root applied outermost so roomscale translation is interpreted in the body's local frame:

    world_camera_eye = body_root · neck/head_attach_offset · hmd_head_pose · (±IPD/2 along head-local X)
    view_eye         = inverse(world_camera_eye)      (paired with that eye's asymmetric projection — vr-runtime blocker 1)

The key move is the yaw split: body yaw comes from the stick (C) or the vehicle/flight root, while head pitch, roll, and residual yaw come only from the HMD, coupled through the deadzone/catch-up above. Head pitch and roll must never feed back into body rotation — tilting your head must not roll the character. Yaw is the only shared axis. `eye_to_head` is a pure ±IPD/2 translation along head-local X with no rotation; each eye still needs its own asymmetric projection.

### Absolute vs. relative placement (gameplay vs. loading)

The chain above is **absolute** placement: in gameplay (`GameState::E_GAME_RUN`) the mod owns the camera position outright, deriving it from Rico's animated head-bone anchor plus the offsets. This is correct while the player owns the world, but it soft-locks a fast-travel teleport: pinning the sim-facing camera (the `GameCameraManager` control contexts and `GetCameraMatrix`) and driving Rico's IK fights the engine's own reset/reposition, and the loading screen hangs forever (issue #27).

**What the engine does during a load.** On entering the load substate (`CGameStateLoad`), the engine calls `CGameCameraManager::ResetCamera` — stamping a character/spawn transform into the active camera — then `SetActive(0)`, which stops the camera tree from updating (`UpdateRenderContexts` is gated on `m_Active`), though `PushRenderContext` keeps re-stamping that parked transform every frame. The load completes on streaming/spawn/landscape/the teleport flag, **not** on any camera convergence. So the camera during a load is a *parked character/spawn transform* — not a usable camera frame — and there is no 3D scene: the loading screen is Scaleform UI, shown in VR on the floating HUD panel (`current_mode()` returns `Movie` outside `E_GAME_RUN`).

So outside `E_GAME_RUN` the mod **leaves the sim camera entirely alone** and head-tracks only the render copy:

- **The sim camera is untouched.** `Camera::UpdateRender` stops all absolute placement (no `m_TransformT0`/`m_TransformT1` write, no render-context patch, no `GetCameraMatrix` override) and touches nothing else; the character/IK hooks stop driving Rico. This is critical: the engine parks the active camera *at the teleport destination* and streams the world from it, so writing that camera — with Rico's head bone, or any frozen pose — feeds the streaming/LOD system the wrong position and the load never completes. (An earlier attempt that wrote a frozen last-gameplay pose into the sim camera re-introduced exactly this hang.)
- The mod holds a **frozen upright loading base**: the last gameplay viewpoint's position and yaw (`hooks::camera::LOADING_BASE`), captured every gameplay frame and held frozen through the transition. Pitch and roll are dropped so the base is always comfortable.
- **The render camera copy head-tracks around that base.** In the `SetupRenderCamera` hook — which runs on the per-frame render copy (`engine+0x170`), *not* the sim camera the streaming system reads — the mod overwrites `m_TransformF`/`m_View` with the cockpit-frame HMD pose (`headpose::xr::cockpit_pose`) composed onto the frozen base, before the engine builds the view-projections. The per-eye IPD and canting apply on top. This changes only what is displayed, so the player looks around a stable viewpoint while the teleport completes underneath. On flatscreen there is no cockpit pose, so the render copy is left as the engine made it.
- The **loading panel world-locks** to the head-tracked render pose: rather than following the head, it latches — on the first load frame — to wherever the player was looking as the load began, then holds that world spot so the player can look around it. The gameplay HUD is the only head-following element. The panel's world-lock and the render camera's head-track share one predicate — `hooks::camera::vr_loading_view_active` (non-gameplay, VR source, base captured) — so they can never disagree on when the loading presentation is active.

On resume, absolute placement snaps back onto Rico's updated head via the freshly captured anchor (the anchor capture keeps running through the load), the panel reverts to head-follow, and the optional auto-recenter (`vr.auto_recenter_on_gameplay`) re-neutralizes once the entry pose settles, mirroring resume-from-menu.

## Boneworks / Bonelab alignment

Boneworks and Bonelab (Stress Level Zero's "Marrow" body) are the closest mechanical analogues, and the useful lesson isn't their physics ragdoll — it's the control *topology*: the head is ground truth, the body hangs from the head, and the body's facing is derived only from head and hands (SLZ: "we only know where your headset is, and where your hands are — nothing else has an accurate direction"). They never auto-align the torso to the travel direction.

We borrow that topology and implement it as kinematic IK on JC3's authored skeleton (we have, and need, no from-scratch physics avatar): the head is ground truth and the authored body hangs under it via a neck/spine offset (head-relative-to-body IK); roomscale XZ translation feeds the character root / collision capsule, so physically walking, leaning, and ducking move the in-game body — on-foot the in-game body matches the player's physical position; and vehicles seat-lock the body to the vehicle while the head stays free (parent the rig to the vehicle transform).

On the camera-on-the-head question we take Boneworks' stance. The character's head bone is the camera's source of truth, and we drive it toward the player's real head — hard, so it tracks 1:1 and stays comfortable (with no physics body yet, nothing pushes it off the real head). Bonelab's biggest vehicle complaint is also instructive: never let the turn input bleed into steering, so keep view and steering decoupled.

## Pitfalls

- **No synthetic neck model on 6DoF.** The rig's head-bone parent already provides the pivot; adding a neck model on top double-counts the motion. (Such models exist for 3DoF phone VR — disable on 6DoF.)
- **No smoothing on the HMD→camera path.** Any lag between the rendered viewpoint and real head motion is nauseating. Body yaw can be smoothed; the head path must stay direct.
- **Baked head animation fights the HMD** — the biggest engineering gotcha for a rigged-character game. JC3's rig drives the head bone (idle sway, look-at, cutscene turns, recoil); in first person the HMD must own head orientation and roomscale position, so suppress or blend out the head-bone animation, or apply it only below the neck. This is exactly what UEVR's Freeze Rotation/Position toggles exist for, and it is the key RE target here: find where the rig writes the head bone / camera so it can be overridden.
- **Positional tracking through geometry.** Roomscale head translation is physical and can't be hard-collided — freezing the view while the real head moves is itself nauseating. Mitigate with body placement (so the head rarely reaches walls) and a fade on deep penetration.

## RE notes (release i64)

The camera hook writes position only — the translation columns of `m_TransformF` — plus a hardcoded ~90° FOV; rotation is never written. Before writing rotation, clear the coordinate-frame gate (the §15.7 experiment in `docs/mod/vr-runtime.md`, blocker 3). Vehicles take a different path (see `docs/engine/rendering.md` §2.8, "Vehicle camera path"), so vehicle head-look behaves differently from on-foot — confirm the cockpit head-bone position is sane (not clipped to seat or world origin) by logging `m_CameraTransform` translation against character world position in a vehicle.

## Hiding the head (and keeping its shadow)

The camera sits at the player's head, so the head mesh — face, eyes, hair, and any gear weighted to facial bones — must be hidden from the player's own view. It is hidden from the colour passes only: the shadow passes see the real skinning data, so Rico's shadow keeps its head. The implementation lives in `payload/src/hooks/graphics_engine/render_block.rs`; the render-block classes, their `Draw`/`DrawZ`/`SetMatrixPalette` entry points, and the batch/palette structures are in pyxis-defs (`rendering/render_block.pyxis`).

The engine facts that shape the design:

- The same render blocks are drawn for every pass; each `Draw` branches on `rc->m_RenderStatus & 6` (`RENDERSTATUS_STATIC_SHADOWMAP | RENDERSTATUS_DYNAMIC_SHADOWMAP`) to take the shadow/depth-only path. There is no separate shadow render block for characters.
- The skinning palette is built once per frame (`CCharacter::UpdateRender` → `MakeSkinningPalette`, `0x140_C3A_FF0`) and shared across all passes, so nothing bone- or palette-side can differ between the shadow and colour passes. Rebuilding the palette between the shadow commit (`CShadowManager::CommitRenderPassSettings`, `0x140_177_9C0`) and the GBuffer draw was considered and rejected: the rebuild is thread-unsafe against the sim thread's pose access. Per-draw spoofing of the palette *upload* sidesteps this entirely.
- Rico's skin material packs the face, arms, and hands into a single render block — and even a single 70-bone skin batch — so neither block-level nor batch-level draw skipping can remove the head without the hands. The hide must be per bone.

The mechanism: the `Draw`/`DrawZ` hooks of `RenderBlockCharacter` and `RenderBlockCharacterSkin` raise a thread-local flag while one of the *player's* blocks draws in a *non-shadow* pass, and the `SetMatrixPalette` hooks then substitute a thread-local copy of the bone matrices with the HEAD and facial bones collapsed — a zero rotation with the translation set to the neck's model-space position, so every weighted vertex contracts to a point inside the collar. The real palette is never modified. The pieces, each forced by an artifact observed in testing:

- **Ownership by instance-info pointer.** Every block draw receives its owning model instance's embedded `CRBIInfo` as the `info` argument (`CModelInstance::AddToPass` passes `instance + 0x50`). The character hook publishes the player's eight `CAnimatedModel::m_ModelInstances` info pointers slot-for-slot, and the draw hooks compare `info` against slot 0 (the body model) for an exact identity test. A position-radius test false-matched nearby NPCs and lost the player at wingsuit speed; matching any slot instead of the body slot alone crumpled the parachute canopy, which lives in another slot with its own skeleton and clashing bone indices.
- **The collapse set** is published by the character hook from the live skeleton: HEAD plus the named facial bones (`offset_facialOrienter`, `fJaw`, lips, nose, lids, brows, eyes, ears) via `GetBoneIndex(hashlittle(name))`. The root and implausible indices are filtered so a failed lookup can never collapse the whole body.
- **Thread-local spoofing.** Character draws run concurrently on multiple render worker threads; a shared flag/buffer leaked the spoof into other characters' draws. `SetMatrixPalette` reads `matrices[BatchToSkeletonLookup[i]]` per batch slot, so the copy spans the batch's highest referenced skeleton index and rewrites only the collapse set.
- **Whole-batch collapse for face gear.** A batch that is mostly facial bones is face *gear* (eyewear and the like), sometimes also rigged to a limb bone for handling animations and blend-anchored on the root; collapsing only its facial bones left the rest dangling mid-body. When ≥ 50% of a batch's non-root bones are in the collapse set, every bone the batch references — including the root, safe because the upload is per-batch — collapses. Mixed batches (the body skin) keep the selective collapse so the hands stay.
- **The collapse target comes from the skeleton, not the palette.** The character hook publishes the neck joint's model-space position right after `CalculateModelSpacePose`. Sampling a position out of the palette instead is unsound: where a matrix's translation lives depends on the block's layout (below), and reading the wrong slots yields a pseudo-point (observed as collapsed clusters parked ~0.6 m below the head with blended vertices streaking down toward them). A single shared neck target also tucks the stretch cones — vertices blended between collapsed and kept bones — into the collar instead of dangling off the chin.
- **Per-batch palette layout detection.** The palette layout differs per block: some store four 3-float columns with the translation in floats `[9..12]`, others three 4-float rows with the translation in each row's fourth element (`[3]`, `[7]`, `[11]`). The tell was the root bone's identity matrix reading `(0, 1, 0)` at `[9..11]` — the middle row, not a translation. Writing through the wrong layout puts the target into the rotation and crushes geometry toward the ground. The hook scores the 3x3 under both readings for orthonormality across up to eight referenced bones and collapses through whichever layout wins; a zero rotation maps every weighted vertex exactly to the translation under either convention.

Reflections and other non-shadow passes inherit the hide (headless in mirrors). A `render_block_dump` diagnostic (the "Dump character block draws" button in the debug UI's Camera tab) logs each nearby character draw's class, pass status, instance transform, ownership slot, and per-batch skeleton lookups, plus each spoofed batch's chosen target and detected layout — the ground truth that settled the ownership, indexing, and layout questions above.

## Simulated headpose (flatscreen)

The headpose abstraction is implemented as two cleanly separated layers:

- **`payload/src/headpose/mod.rs`** — the pure pose source of truth. Owns the current `HeadPose { position, orientation }`, the previous-tick pose (for the engine's T0/T1 interpolation, below), and the head-bone anchor. No latch logic, no mode detection. The sim (or VR) writes via `set_pose()`; consumers (head bone, camera, HUD) read via `query()`/`query_prev()`. This is the VR seam: when OpenXR lands, the sim is replaced with the HMD pose and nothing else changes.

- **`payload/src/headpose/sim.rs`** — the flatscreen simulation. Owns mouse-look input accumulation, the latch state machine, mode detection, body-yaw target computation, and the per-frame `update()` that produces a `HeadPose` and publishes it via `set_pose()`.

The latch mechanism is a sim concern: in VR, the player's head and body are independent (head via HMD, body via stick). In flatscreen, the latch unifies them — within a configurable cone the head moves freely (decoupled), past the threshold the body yaw tracks the head direction. This coupling lives entirely in the sim; the headpose abstraction knows nothing about it. The sim's yaw is **body-relative** (composed with the live body yaw at publish time): on foot the offset is compensated as the body turns, so the head stays world-anchored while the body catches up and the latch converges; in vehicles the offset rides the body frame, giving cockpit-relative free-look. Recentering is likewise a source concern — the sim recenters by zeroing its body-relative angles; the VR source will own its own re-basing.

Input capture uses the game's own look effectors: `InputDeviceManager::Update` is hooked post-call to read and consume the `LOOK_LEFT`/`RIGHT`/`UP`/`DOWN` effector values, feeding them into the sim. The game's camera never sees the input (effectors are cleared post-poll). The poll runs on the engine's fixed-rate sim tick, not per rendered frame (`CGame`'s "engine %d hz - %d upd, 1 rend" decoupling), so look deltas arrive in tick-sized bursts; the whole sim step runs *inside* the hook, so the published previous/current pose pair rotates on the engine's own tick timeline, phase-aligned with its dtf reset. (Deferring the step to the next frame left the render lerping a stale pair at dtf ≈ 0 on every tick frame — a per-tick jerk.)

The head bone override in `UpdatePropEffects` first captures the *animated* head joint (via `GetJoint`, which at that point holds this frame's freshly computed model-space pose) and publishes its world position as the headpose anchor, then rotates the HEAD bone by the sim's body-relative angles *composed onto the animated model-space orientation* — an absolute orientation write assumed the bone rest frame matched the model axes, which it does not (the shadow showed the head collapsing into the shoulders) — keeping the animated translation plus the roomscale offset. The legacy scale-hide (HEAD plus a facial-bone list to 0.001) is a default-off fallback, superseded by the render-block palette collapse (see "Hiding the head" below), which hides the head from the colour passes while the shadow keeps it. The anchor must come from the pre-override joint: reading the bone matrices instead returns last frame's override and closes a feedback loop that freezes the position at a fixed world point (and stretches the head toward it as the body walks away).

The camera hook (`camera_update_render`) writes rotation + translation to `m_TransformT0`/`m_TransformT1` **before** calling the original, because `Camera::UpdateRender` itself lerps T0 → T1 by `dtf` into `m_TransformF` and derives `m_View = inverse(m_TransformF)` — post-call writes do nothing for the current frame and are clobbered by the next frame's `PushRenderContext`/`InitTransform`. T0 gets the previous-tick pose and T1 the current one, so the engine's own lerp smooths the tick-rate headpose across rendered frames; writing at this seam also bypasses `PushRenderContext`'s rotation jitter filter (`s_use_jitter_filter`), which otherwise snaps sub-epsilon rotation deltas and quantizes head motion into visible steps. `CameraTree::UpdateRenderContexts` patches the control contexts with the same previous/current split; the next render context also feeds `GameCameraManager::GetInputMatrix`, so movement-direction mapping follows the headpose.

Mode detection uses the `ORIENTATION_EVAL_CALLS` counter (the `EvaluateCharacterOrientation` detours, counted for the local player before any config gating): it advances every on-foot *sim tick* including idle, unlike the move/aim task counters, which stop while idle and would clamp the head to the free-look cone whenever the player stood still. The comparison runs once per input tick, not per rendered frame — the counter only moves on sim ticks, so a per-frame comparison flickered to "not on foot" on every frame without a tick, resetting the latch and leaking the idle camera pin through. On foot with the headpose active, the face-camera idle pin is suppressed (the camera forward *is* the head forward, so the pin would defeat the decoupled cone); the latch's body-yaw target takes priority over the pin and applies regardless of the face-camera toggle.

The published orientation composes the body-relative offset onto the body's *full* world rotation, not just its yaw, so the body's pitch and roll carry the head with them — a banking wingsuit rolls the view (`docs/mod/head-and-body.md`'s roll-with-the-bank default). The root rotation alone misses animation-driven posture: hanging, ledge grabs, and similar authored animations invert the body in the *bone pose* over a root that stays gravity-aligned (the wingsuit works because its bank rotates the root itself). The sim therefore folds in a **posture swing** — the rotation taking body-up to the animated neck axis (measured from the head/neck anchor translations, whose conventions are proven in-game, rather than joint orientations, whose rest frames are not) — with a deadband so idle sway and locomotion lean never wobble the view, ramping to full past a configurable deviation, and an exponential low-pass (~0.5 s) on top: the raw per-tick swing carries the walk cycle's torso oscillation, and near full inversion its axis comes from a near-zero cross product and flaps tick to tick (in-game this read as violent swinging while walking and a wildly spinning yaw while hanging). The smoothed swing keeps only the low-frequency component — a hang settles into the inverted view over the time constant. The swing is twist-free (a single-axis alignment); at exact inversion the axis is ambiguous and a pitch flip is chosen. **Off by default**: even smoothed, the measurement needs more dialling in (and likely a proper bone-basis treatment, which the queued neck-drive work below will want anyway) before it reads well — until then the view deliberately stays upright during animation-driven inversion.

Extended yaw range: the free-look yaw limit defaults to ±135°, made anatomically plausible by driving the *neck* bone — body-relative yaw beyond a configurable start (60°) twists the neck along with the head, up to a cap (55°). The twist pre-multiplies the animated model-space neck orientation with a model-space Y rotation (body-relative yaw *is* a model-space Y rotation), so no bone rest-frame knowledge is needed and the neck's translation is untouched. Eventually the shoulders/spine should share the twist too. The camera's eye offset pivots about the animated *neck* bone (the character hook publishes the head-to-neck delta alongside the anchor), so pitching the head swings the eyes forward over the chest and looking down clears the body instead of staring into the neck. With `use_eye_matrices` on, the pivot arm's base is the *measured* body-frame neck-to-eye-midpoint arm from the animated eye bones, and the camera config's `head_offset` is a correction on top; with it off, `head_offset` is the whole arm. This is a sim-only approximation of the neck's kinematics — the "no synthetic neck model" pitfall applies to VR, where the HMD provides the real translation and this pivot is replaced wholesale by the tracked pose (issue #12). With the headpose active, the whole camera placement (position and orientation) comes from the tick-spaced pose pair — the anchor plus the configured head/body offsets — rather than from per-frame bone reads: `GetSafeBoneMatrix`/`GetJoint` only carry the finalized sim-rate pose, so bone-derived placement steps at the tick rate even though the mesh itself interpolates via the skinning-palette pose pair (issue #20), and the eye-bone average additionally no longer lands where the eyes render once the head bone is player-driven.

**The aim seam.** The game splits its camera getters by phase — see `docs/engine/aim-pipeline.md`'s "The camera getters" for the full `GetInputMatrix`/`GetCameraMatrix`/`GetAlternateAimMatrix` split and which sim-side consumers (`CPlayerAimControl`, the weapon aim-target queries, the melee and grapple tasks) read `GetCameraMatrix`. In short: `GetCameraMatrix` reads a sim-phase context that, once the render-phase patch has consumed the look input, is frozen at its injection-time direction. The mod hooks `GetCameraMatrix` post-call and overwrites the output with the render camera's headpose transform, so every sim-side aim consumer follows the HMD gaze instead of that stale internal camera.

That override has a side effect on the body — see `docs/engine/aim-pipeline.md`'s "Body-turn reactions to the aim direction" for how `NStateTask_LocoUtil::GetAimMoveAngle` and the aim-relative `track_face_dir` blackboard turn the body toward the aim target natively — so the game itself turned the body toward the head long before the latch threshold. The mod hooks `GetAimMoveAngle` and returns zero (reads as "already aligned") for the local player while the headpose has the head decoupled on foot, idle, and not really aiming; in every other case the original runs, so when the latch engages the game's own turn machinery performs the catch-up toward the head natively. In the same decoupled-idle state the `force_face_camera` shim forces `track_face_dir` off rather than passing the game's value through, since the executor's continuous yaw would otherwise turn the body with the head even with the turn acts suppressed.

## Body IK via HumanIK

The body-hangs-from-the-head topology above is implemented on JC3's authored skeleton through the engine's own HumanIK solver rather than a bespoke IK pass, so the spine and neck bend toward where the player looks the same way the game's aim IK bends them. The full solver layout, per-frame lifecycle, effector-id model, and coordinate-space recipe are in `docs/engine/humanik.md`; this is the head-and-body view of it.

A pre-call hook on `Character::UpdatePassFinalizePose_Parallel` (`payload/src/hooks/character.rs`) queues a head effector *position* target — and optionally a rotation target — on the `MAIN` pass at the `UPPER_BODY` solve step, before the engine's `HasTargets` gate, so a single head target pulls the whole upper body toward the headpose that same frame. The target is the headpose position transformed into character-model space (the space `AddEffectorTargetPosition` expects), and only the head effector's target and reach-weight slots are written, so this coexists with the game's own aim/hand targets on the same pass (the pass's step is the max of all queued targets). The engine's global-HIK enable and the per-character reduced-LOD gates are respected exactly as the engine checks them, so nothing is queued in a frame the solver would not run.

This is the seam the earlier "no synthetic neck model" and "baked head animation fights the HMD" pitfalls point at. The anchor capture moved into this same pre-IK hook so the headpose anchor reads the freshly animated (pre-solve) pose and can never chase the target the solver pulls it toward — a feedback loop that would pin the camera to a fixed world point while the body walks out from under it. The `SetJoint`-based head-bone override still runs at the very end of the pass (`UpdatePropEffects`), on top of the HIK-bent spine, and composes its body-relative offset onto a *pre-IK* orientation snapshot when body IK is on, so it does not double-count the yaw the solver already applied. HumanIK owns the spine/neck bend; the override owns the final head orientation.

The `body_ik` config section (`payload/src/config.rs`, live-editable from the debug UI's Camera and VR tabs) exposes the translation- and rotation-reach weights, a master weight, the interpolation and blend-out rates (the game's own hand-pass defaults), an optional rotation target, and a model-space target offset. It is **off by default**: the interaction with the game's own chest/spine aim targets and the propagation of the solved head into the later `SetJoint` override are the open risks catalogued in `docs/engine/humanik.md`, and want in-headset validation before it ships on.

## Suppressing idle motion

The head override and body IK place the head and bend the spine, but the authored animation underneath still plays Rico's idle motion while standing still, and with the head owned by the HMD and the body meant to hold the player's real pose, that motion reads as the torso drifting independently of the player (issue #33) — the same class of problem as "baked head animation fights the HMD" one layer down. It has **two distinct sources**, addressed separately.

**The fidget** — the periodic weight-shift/look-around. On foot the character cycles between two afsmb rule states, `S_IDLE` (the resting base) and `S_IDLE_ONE_OFF` (a one-off idle break); `NStateTask_InputLocoIdleTask::Update` drives the transition, counting down a per-character timer (`character + 0x2BB0`, reset to a random ~8.5–12 s whenever the aim reference moves) and, once it elapses, queuing `ACT_TO_IDLE_ONE_OFF` to enter the one-off. The mod suppresses this by hooking the idle task and holding that countdown positive for the local player (`payload/src/hooks/input/locomotion.rs`), so the one-off branch never fires. Holding the timer — rather than swallowing the act at `QueueAct` — is deliberate: the task `return`s right after queuing, *before* its walk/start-act queuing, so a swallowed act with a stuck-negative timer leaves the task perpetually short-circuiting and Rico loses his movement animations while idle. Gated by `movement.suppress_idle_fidget` (default on).

**The breathing** — the subtle chest/shoulder motion baked into the base `S_IDLE` clip, which keeps advancing as long as the clip's clock runs. There is no act to drop; the mod instead holds the animation clock. `CCharacter::UpdatePassAnimations_Parallel` advances the character's clips each SIM frame through `AnimationController::UpdateAnimationsTime` and `UpdateAnimations` (both ultimately `CBlender::UpdateTime(dt)`); the mod hooks both and passes `dt = 0` while the controller is the local player's *and* that player's active rule state is `S_IDLE` (`payload/src/hooks/animation.rs`), so the pose freezes at its current frame. `dt = 0` is the same value the engine feeds when the game is paused, so it is a safe, well-trodden path. Movement and every other state run at full `dt`, and NPCs are gated out by a controller-pointer compare before any work. Gated by `movement.suppress_idle_breathing` (**off by default**, pending in-headset validation — a held pose is a larger visual change than dropping the fidget).

The active rule state is read by walking `CCharacter`'s first state-machine instance to its current state and hashing nothing — the state carries `hashlittle(state_name)` at `+0x10`, matched against `hashlittle("S_IDLE")` (the engine's `HashString` *is* `hashlittle`).

Two dead ends are worth recording so they are not re-explored. The ambient idle is **not** an additive layer here: `CMiscAdditiveState` (the additive-layer applier) is never active during on-foot `S_IDLE`, so zeroing its sampler weights does nothing, and the engine's designer-facing `Dev|Character|Override Additive Animation Layer` toggle is **dead in the release build** — with its debug menu compiled out, `COverrideAdditiveAnimLayerCondition::Evaluate` constant-folds to always-false (verified in the release decompile). And `S_IDLE` is not backed by `CIdleState`, so hooking individual state classes' `UpdateSamplers` missed it; reading the active rule state and gating the clock is the altitude that works.
