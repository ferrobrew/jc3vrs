# Floating HUD in VR

The flat game draws its HUD — health, ammo, minimap, reticle, objective markers, distance labels — straight onto the final LDR image (rendering §10). In VR that would pin the whole HUD to the screen plane at infinity, which is unreadable and uncomfortable. Instead the mod renders the HUD into its own texture, then draws that texture as a quad floating a couple of metres in front of the camera, damped so it follows the head without feeling nailed to it. World-anchored markers are a separate problem, handled by reprojection rather than by the panel — see "World-to-screen" below.

## Presentation: in-engine quad now, OpenXR layer later

There are two ways to present the HUD texture in 3D. An **OpenXR quad layer** (`XrCompositionLayerQuad`) hands the runtime the texture plus a pose and a size; the compositor draws it once at display rate, reprojected every frame and sampled directly — sharpest text, robust to game-framerate drops. An **in-scene quad** is one we draw into each eye ourselves, inside the stereo render.

The mod uses the in-scene quad. It lets distance, size, and follow-lag be dialled in against the existing desktop stereo render — the side-by-side preview — with no headset in the loop. A quad layer can only be seen in a headset, so it can't be iterated on the desktop, and no OpenXR runtime is wired up yet; the in-scene quad shows up in the preview immediately. Its downsides (the compositor double-samples it, so text is slightly softer; it's coupled to game framerate) don't matter while tuning, and we need an in-scene quad for world markers regardless. The quad layer stays on the table as a later swap for final sharpness once we're in-headset.

## Redirecting the HUD into our texture

The `CUIManager` singleton (the Scaleform GFx UI manager) renders into the engine's back buffer by default. `CUIManager::InitPlatformRT` is where it binds its render target: it builds a Scaleform render buffer (`m_RenderBuffer`), pulls the RTV and DSV from the engine surface (`GetRTVFromSurface` / `GetDSVFromSurface`), and binds them via `RenderTargetData::UpdateData`. It runs once at startup and again on every device or resolution reset (`InitializeSystem` / `RestoreAfterReset`).

The mod redirects the HUD by calling `RenderTargetData::UpdateData` itself (`payload/src/hud/binding.rs`): it creates its own offscreen HUD texture with an `ID3D11RenderTargetView` and depth-stencil view over it, then calls `UpdateData(m_RenderBuffer's inner RenderTargetData, myRTV, nullptr, myDSV)` to swap the UI's bound RTV/DSV to our texture. `UpdateData` refcounts the views it swaps (releases the old, AddRefs the new), so it's safe to call standalone. Because the rebind isn't tied to startup, it works at any time — a late-injected mod (where `InitPlatformRT` has already run) just calls `UpdateData` once after creating its texture, with no dependency on `InitPlatformRT` firing again. The mod does **not** hook `InitPlatformRT`; the redirect path does call it to rebuild `m_RenderBuffer` and reflow the movie rectangle to the texture's aspect (patching `m_BufferHeight` / `m_ViewRectBottom` for a non-square texture), then calls `UpdateData` to point the views at our texture.

The redirect installs lazily on the render thread and re-applies on a resolution change (a back-buffer size change means the engine re-ran `InitPlatformRT` and discarded our rebind). Disabling the redirect — or unloading the mod — restores the engine's own binding by re-running `InitPlatformRT` so the UI never renders into a freed texture.

Once the HUD renders into our offscreen RTV instead of the engine's working surface, it's **automatically excluded from the scene composite** — `HandleDrawThreadTask`'s `CopySurfaceToTexture` resolves the working surface to the presentable, and the HUD no longer being on that surface means no separate composite-suppression patch is needed (rendering §12). The mod then samples our texture as a 3D quad. `CUIManager::RenderOffScreenTextures` is *not* the tool here — it's for registered in-world movie screens, not the HUD.

## Comfort: lazy follow

A HUD rigidly locked to the head is the worst case — the eyes can't settle on it and head tremor is amplified. World-locked is the other extreme — it slides out of view the moment you turn to fight. The comfortable middle is a delayed, lazy follow: the panel eases toward a head-relative target, with position decoupled from orientation.

The panel sits at a configurable distance (3 m default, inside the 1.5–3 m comfort band — far enough to avoid vergence strain, near enough to read), flat. The gameplay HUD panel eases toward the head's current orientation with critically-damped quaternion slerp, with no deadzone; position is de-jittered separately. Because the orientation follow is a single slerp toward the head quaternion, it tracks yaw, pitch, and roll together. Roll matters for JC3: parachute, wingsuit, and vehicle sections all bank heavily, and VR head-tracking adds more. Without a deadzone the panel always tracks the head; the halflife alone controls how snappy the follow is. A quick flick barely moves the panel because the halflife limits how far it can travel in a short interval, but the panel never gets stuck at a permanent offset from where the head is looking.

The damper is a critically-damped exponential, so it converges fast with no overshoot and is frame-rate independent: `alpha = 1 - 2^(-dt/halflife); current = slerp(current, target, alpha)` (Holden's damper). The follow has two halflives, both exposed as sliders: a rotation halflife (0.2 s default) and a position de-jitter halflife (0.1 s default). See `payload/src/hud/config.rs` and `payload/src/hud/state.rs`.

The panel has two modes (`HudMode`, chosen per frame by `current_mode`): the gameplay HUD, which head-follows as above, and full-screen UI — movies, loading screens, and menus — which is world-static, latched in place so the head can look away from it. Each mode carries its own aspect (`hud_aspect` / `movie_aspect`). The world-static latch is currently disabled (`WORLD_STATIC_MOVIE_PANEL = false`): outside gameplay the render camera has no valid pose, so the latch would capture a degenerate transform and strand the panel off-screen. Until the head can be driven from the player's pose (issue #5), the full-screen panel head-follows like the gameplay HUD and only the aspect differs.

## Stereo depth: one panel, projected per eye

The panel sits at a finite world depth with correct stereo disparity. Its world-space corners are computed once per frame on eye 0 (from the chosen pose, distance, and effective aspect) and cached; both eyes then project those same world-space corners through their own per-eye view-projection. Computing the corners per-eye instead would cancel the world transform against the per-eye view and collapse the panel to view space — head-locked, zero disparity, at infinity. The panel resizes with distance to keep a constant apparent (angular) size, so the distance slider (0.3–10 m) can be changed freely without the HUD growing or shrinking; the physical size derives from the size knob, the distance, and the aspect (`hud::panel_height`).

## World-to-screen: split the HUD

This is the crux. The game places objective markers, enemy pips, and distance labels by projecting a world point through the camera's view-projection to a screen coordinate. If we baked that into the lagging panel texture, a marker that should point 20° to the left would end up wherever the panel happened to have drifted. The HUD is split by what each element actually *means*:

- **Static HUD** — health, ammo, minimap, reticle frame — has no world anchor. It's drawn into the panel texture at its native flat positions and lags with the panel. That lag is correct and comfortable, and no world-to-screen is involved.
- **World-anchored markers** mean a *direction*, not a panel position. They must not be baked into a lagging screen-plane projection. The mod reprojects them onto the panel's surface: `CUIManager::Get2DInfo` is hooked (`payload/src/hooks/ui.rs`) and its VP and camera matrix are replaced with the floating panel's orientation (`hud::compute_panel_vp`), so a marker lands at the correct spot on the panel even after follow-damping lag. The panel VP uses the damped follow rotation for orientation and the active camera's position and projection, re-aspected to the panel.

The engine makes this directly doable because its world-to-screen takes the VP as a parameter. `CUIManager::Convert3DCoords` projects a world point through a supplied `vp`, divides by w, aspect-corrects, and maps NDC to pixels, returning false when the point is behind the camera. `Get2DInfo` forwards a caller-supplied `vp` to it and handles the on-screen test and edge-clamping (`ClampToScreen`). Because `Convert3DCoords`' aspect correction reads `m_CachedViewportRatio` (the window height/width, refreshed every frame), the hook temporarily sets it to the panel's `height / width` (= `1 / aspect`) for the call and restores it afterwards, so other world-to-screen consumers in the same frame are unaffected. When the redirect or quad is off, the original caller's VP and camera are passed through unchanged. The default VP is the render camera's `m_ViewProjectionF`.

One consumer bypasses `Get2DInfo` entirely: the grapple reticle projects through a default-VP wrapper (`CUIManager::Convert3DCoordsDefault`, whose only callers are in `CHUDUI::UpdateGrappleReticle`). That wrapper is hooked separately (`payload/src/hooks/ui.rs`) to route it through the same panel reprojection, and doubles as the source of the aim depth below.

## Depth layers: the multi-pass split

With one HUD texture, every element shares the panel's depth: a crosshair crossing a distant marker gets the marker's disparity, and Scaleform has already composited overlapping clips into the texture, so nothing texture-side can separate them. The split (`hud.split`, `payload/src/hud/split.rs`) renders the HUD three times per frame with different subsets visible, into three textures, and composites each at its own depth:

- **Static** — the six corner/edge safe-area containers and the weapon-selection wheel — at the panel distance, as before.
- **Markers** — `MCI_poi_stage` (all POIs, including the auto-aim marker) and `MCI_hud` (the target-tracker stage) — at its own distance, or per-marker depths with the warp below.
- **Center** — `MCI_safe_area_center`: the weapon/grapple/mech reticles, pickups, and center indicators — at the aim depth.

The mechanism lives in a `CUIManager::Render` detour. `Render` draws the movie's latest *captured* display-tree snapshot, so a visibility change only takes effect through a fresh `Movie::Capture`; each pass therefore writes its partition's `_visible` flags on the AS3 `MovieRoot`, captures (after borrowing capture-thread ownership the way the engine's own `RenderOffScreenTextures` does), rebinds the UI render buffer's views to the pass's texture via `RenderTargetData::UpdateData`, and calls the original `Render`. The whole sequence holds `m_DeferredRenderLock` — the same lock `PreRender` holds across `Advance`+`Capture` — so the update thread cannot mutate the display list mid-split, and the critical section re-enters cleanly when the original takes it. Every frame ends restored to all-visible with capture ownership handed back, so menus and a mid-frame disable always see the full HUD. Any missing precondition degrades to the single-texture render and logs once.

The composite draws the layers farthest-first (alpha-blended overlays without a depth test, so painter's order decides occlusion between layers), each with its own world-space corner set at the panel's constant apparent size — moving a layer changes only its stereo depth. The clip paths are the authored top-level names of `hud.gfx`; the runtime attachment prefix under the root movie is configurable (`hud.split_path_prefix`) until the in-game display-tree dump (the HUD tab's Scaleform section, `payload/src/hud/scaleform.rs`) pins it.

**Per-marker depth (the warp).** The `Get2DInfo` hook records each on-screen marker's panel UV and its world point's distance from the panel anchor. The marker layer then draws as a grid mesh whose vertex shader (`payload/src/shaders/hud_layer_vs.hlsl`) displaces every vertex along its ray from the panel anchor by a marker-blended depth field: each point stays on its own anchor ray, so the cyclopean image is identical regardless of the field — only stereo disparity varies, giving each marker's neighborhood the disparity of the thing it labels. Depths blend in disparity (1/d) space with a radial falloff per marker and an edge fade; edge-clamped markers (directional indicators, not world points) stay at the layer's base depth. Overlapping markers blend to a disparity-weighted compromise — the texture already composited them, so that is the best any single-texture approach can do.

**Aim depth.** The grapple-reticle hook records the aim point's distance; the center layer's distance eases toward it (critically-damped, 0.15 s halflife) and settles back to the configured distance when nothing has been targeted for half a second, so the reticle sits at the vergence of the surface it targets.

**Overlay suppression (issue #8).** While splitting, `hud.suppress_overlays` holds the full-screen overlay clips — the drowning tint, the omni-damage flash and health paint, and both directional damage-indicator containers — invisible in every pass.

## Status and future work

Shipped: the redirect, the in-scene floating quad per eye, the lazy-follow damping with sliders, constant-apparent-size scaling, and the per-eye marker reprojection. Manual distance control is a slider.

Implemented but pending in-game verification (issue #14 branch): the multi-pass depth split, the per-marker warp, the aim-driven center depth, and the overlay suppression — see `docs/issue-08-14-hud-overlays-and-depth.md` for the verification checklist and the reverse-engineering behind it.

Future work: swap the static panel to an `XrCompositionLayerQuad` once in-headset for final sharpness; temporal damping for per-marker depths; automatic panel distance from scene depth; re-enabling the world-static full-screen panel once the head pose is valid outside gameplay (issue #5).
